# Food Guide — Japan 2026

**Group:** 2 adults + 2 kids (7 & 9) + grandma (75) | **Season:** Early June 2026

Focused on: kid-friendly, authentic, not tourist traps. Chains are welcome — Japanese chains are genuinely good and exactly where local families eat.

---

## Curry

### CoCo Ichibanya (CoCo壱番屋) — All Cities
The definitive Japanese curry chain. This is what curry means in Japan. Spice levels run 0–10 — kids get 0 or 1, adults can go as high as they want. Every topping is customizable (tonkatsu, cheese, vegetables, egg). Clean, fast, cheap, and beloved by Japanese families. Not a tourist trap — it's just Tuesday.

- **Where:** Multiple locations in Tokyo (Shinjuku, Ikebukuro, Shibuya), Kyoto (near the station), and a location in Odawara near Hakone
- **Price:** ~800–1,200 yen per person
- **Kid tip:** Order for kids at spice level 0 with a fried chicken or corn topping

### Go Go Curry — Tokyo
Kanazawa-style curry: thick, dark, deeply savory sauce over rice with a crispy tonkatsu cutlet and shredded cabbage. More personality than CoCo, portions are large, prices are low. Popular with salarymen and anyone who wants something hearty.

- **Where:** Multiple Tokyo locations; good branch near Shinjuku Station (south exit area)
- **Price:** ~700–1,000 yen per person
- **Kid tip:** The sauce is mild by default — fine for kids. The katsu is easy to cut up for smaller eaters.

## Sushi

### Sushiro (スシロー) — Tokyo + Kyoto
The #1 kaiten (conveyor belt) sushi chain in Japan by volume. This is where Japanese families actually go. Plates run 100–300 yen each and the fish quality is genuinely impressive for the price. Order via the touchscreen at your table or grab whatever comes by on the belt.

- **Where:** Multiple Tokyo locations (Shibuya, Shinjuku, Ikebukuro); Kyoto locations near the station and in Fushimi
- **Price:** ~1,500–2,500 yen per person
- **Tip:** Download the Sushiro app before arriving in Japan to join the waitlist remotely — lines at dinner move fast

### Kura Sushi — Tokyo
Same kaiten format as Sushiro, similar quality, but with one kid-killer feature: every 5 plates you put into the slot at your table triggers a "bikkura-pon" capsule toy game. Kids 7 & 9 will spend the entire meal strategizing how to maximize their turns. Excellent distraction, excellent sushi.

- **Where:** Multiple Tokyo locations
- **Price:** ~1,500–2,500 yen per person

### Uobei (渋谷魚べい) — Tokyo, Shibuya
Bullet-train sushi: you order on a tablet and your food arrives via a dedicated rail car directly to your seat at high speed. No belt — it comes *to you*, fast. Kids go completely feral for this. The food is solid, the gimmick is real, and nobody leaves unimpressed.

- **Where:** Shibuya (near Shibuya Station, Mark City area); also Shinjuku
- **Price:** ~100–300 yen per plate
- **Tip:** Great on Day 3 (DisneySea day) or Day 5 (Shibuya area) — both are nearby

## Ramen

### Ippudo (一風堂) — Tokyo + Kyoto
High-quality Hakata-style tonkotsu (pork bone broth) ramen. National chain but genuinely excellent — not a concession to tourists. Rich, creamy broth, thin straight noodles, perfectly tender chashu pork. Family-friendly seating throughout (no solo booths). Staff at most locations are comfortable with international visitors.

- **Where:** Multiple Tokyo locations (Roppongi, Shinjuku, Shibuya, Ikebukuro); Kyoto Kawaramachi location
- **Price:** ~1,000–1,500 yen per person
- **Kid tip:** Broth is rich but not spicy by default. Kids typically take to it well. You can ask for a smaller portion (ko-size) at some locations.

### Ichiran (一蘭) — Tokyo
Solo-booth ramen: you sit in an individual partitioned stall, fill out a paper form customizing your broth richness/spice/noodle firmness/etc., and your bowl appears through a bamboo curtain. Tonkotsu broth, excellent quality. The experience is uniquely Japanese and slightly surreal.

- **Where:** Multiple Tokyo locations; Shinjuku, Shibuya, Harajuku
- **Price:** ~1,000–1,300 yen per person
- **Kid note:** The solo booth setup can feel weird for kids used to eating together. Most locations have a few "family seats" where the partitions are removed — ask for these when you arrive. Good for one visit as an experience.

### Honke Daiichiasahi (本家第一旭) — Kyoto
Open since 1947. Simple Kyoto-style ramen: soy-forward pork broth, thin noodles, generous chashu, green onions. A genuine local institution near Kyoto Station with a line that moves quickly. Cheap, no frills, deeply satisfying.

- **Where:** Near Kyoto Station (east side, Toji area) — short walk or taxi
- **Price:** ~800–1,000 yen per person
- **Tip:** Go for lunch on Day 8 (arrival day) or Day 10 (departure morning) — both days you're near Kyoto Station anyway

### Kyoto Ramen Koji — Kyoto Station, 10th Floor
Ten famous ramen shops from around Japan under one roof inside Kyoto Station tower. This sounds like a tourist trap — it's not. Japanese travelers eat here when passing through, and the tenant lineup is carefully curated. Great for trying Kyoto-style (light chicken/soy), Sapporo (miso), or Hakata (tonkotsu) side by side.

- **Where:** Kyoto Station Building, The Cube, 10th floor
- **Price:** ~1,000–1,500 yen per person
- **Tip:** Grandma-friendly — elevator access directly, no hiking required. Good for Day 8 lunch right after shinkansen arrival before checking into Four Seasons.

## Hakone Note

Hakone is a resort area — standalone restaurant options are limited and tend to be expensive. Since your ryokan (Kowakien Tenyu) includes dinner both nights, you mainly need lunch on Days 6 and 7.

**What to do instead:** Hakone-Yumoto (the main station town) has excellent local **soba** shops — this is actually where Hakone shines food-wise. Handmade buckwheat noodles, light dashi broth, very kid-friendly. Look for any of the small shops near Hakone-Yumoto Station; Hatajuku Soba is a local favorite.

**Yunessun area:** If you're hungry mid-day during the water park visit, the resort complex has a food court with ramen, curry, and udon — convenience over quality, but it works.

## Convenience Store (Konbini) — Underrated, Everywhere

Don't overlook 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, and Lawson for snacks, quick breakfasts, and late-night bites. Japanese konbini food is genuinely good:

- **Onigiri** (rice balls) — kids almost always love these; huge variety
- **Tamagoyaki sandwiches** and egg salad sandwiches — a Japanese cult classic
- **Hot foods counter** — karaage chicken, steamed buns, corn dogs
- **Desserts** — pudding cups, cream puffs, strawberry shortcake slices
- There's a 7-Eleven in the basement of your Hyatt Regency Tokyo — extremely convenient for Day 1 jet-lag dinner and any early-morning needs
